The strong move

Get real help

A website can show you the map. It cannot sit with you, prescribe for you, or pull you out of deep water. Humans do that. Here's where they are.

If it's a crisis

If you're thinking about suicide or self-harm, that has its own page, with people who will pick up right now. It's here. Reaching out is not dramatic and not a failure — it's what the strong version of you, looking back from a year out, is begging you to do.

If you're not in crisis, but you're not okay

The tiers, roughly in order of how stuck you are:

A counselor or therapist

For persistent depression, anxiety, trauma, or addiction, this is the front line — not the backup plan. Psychology Today's directory filters by insurance, price, and specialty; many therapists offer sliding-scale rates, and Open Path Collective lists sessions at $40–80. If faith matters to you, you can filter for clinicians who work within it.

A doctor, about your brain

If the gray has lasted months, talk to a physician. Depression and anxiety are medical conditions with medical options. Medication is not a faith failure — you wouldn't pray away a broken leg instead of setting it, and your neurochemistry is not more spiritual than your femur.

Addiction-specific help

If a substance or behavior has you and the 30-day reset keeps failing, that's not weak willpower, that's the disease model being accurate. SAMHSA's helpline (1-800-662-4357) is free and 24/7. Twelve-step groups (AA, NA, and the secular alternatives like SMART Recovery) are free, everywhere, and built on the structured-community mechanics this site keeps pointing at.

A real community

Half the protocols on this site point at the same mechanism: structured, recurring, in-person contact. Anything standing and weekly works — a team, a class, a volunteer crew, a club. If you're open to it, a local church is the oldest delivery system for exactly this; the test is simple: do they know each other's actual lives? Whatever you pick, give it six weeks before judging it.

What this site is, and isn't

uncooked is education and a starting push: pattern recognition, the research behind your rut, and practices with evidence behind them. It is not therapy, diagnosis, medical advice, or pastoral care, and it was not written by clinicians. Everything here works alongside professional help, never instead of it.

One more thing, because someone needs to hear it: getting help early is cheaper, faster, and easier than getting it late. The loop always votes for waiting. Don't let it.
